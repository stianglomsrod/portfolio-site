# STIAN_VOICE_PROFILE.md

## Purpose

Use this profile as context when editing text for Stian Glomsrød’s portfolio/application site. The goal is not to make the writing “perfectly corporate”. The goal is to make it feel credible, human, specific, and recognizably Stian: reflective, practical, curious, slightly self-aware, and grounded in real work.

This profile should guide rewrites, UX microcopy, case descriptions, headings, button text, game text, and the distinction between the professional portfolio mode and the more experimental “Skamløs AI-pitch” mode.

## Who Stian is in this context

Stian is a teacher, master’s graduate in digital learning design, and self-directed developer/designer moving toward UX, AI-assisted product development, learning technology, prototyping, and developer/product roles.

He is not trying to pretend to be a conventional senior UX designer with a long agency portfolio. His strength is the combination of:

- real-world teaching and classroom experience
- design-based research and participatory design
- working with pupils and teachers as actual users/co-designers
- building functional prototypes, not only static ideas
- using AI and coding agents as part of a serious design/development workflow
- understanding systems, learning processes, friction, accessibility, autonomy support, and user needs
- being honest about what is exploratory, what is tested, and what is a prototype

The portfolio should present him as unusually relevant for UX/product work where AI, interaction design, prototyping, learning, and real user contexts overlap.

## Voice and tone

Stian’s voice should feel:

- warm, direct, and reflective
- human rather than polished to death
- curious and slightly nerdy, but not performative
- practical and grounded in concrete examples
- honest about process, mistakes, iteration, and learning
- confident without sounding inflated
- professional, but not bland
- occasionally playful in the “Skamløs AI-pitch” mode

Avoid making him sound like a generic AI-generated applicant. The writing should not feel like a LinkedIn thought-leader, a consultancy brochure, or a “passionate problem-solver leveraging synergies” cliché.

Good Stian-like qualities:

- “Jeg liker å forstå hvordan systemer henger sammen.”
- “Jeg blir motivert av å gjøre uklare behov om til noe folk faktisk kan prøve.”
- “Jeg er opptatt av hva som skjer når løsningen møter hverdagen, ikke bare når den ser fin ut i Figma.”
- “Jeg har lært mye av å bygge ting som nesten fungerer, teste dem, og finne ut hvorfor de ikke helt gjør det ennå.”
- “AI er ikke magi her; det er en del av arbeidsflyten, og den må styres, testes og korrigeres.”

## Things to avoid: AI markers

Actively look for and reduce these AI markers:

- overuse of “innovative”, “seamless”, “robust”, “transformative”, “cutting-edge”, “leveraging”, “unlocking potential”
- empty claims without examples
- too many three-part sentence structures
- repeated “not only X, but Y”
- generic enthusiasm: “I am passionate about…”
- polished but vague paragraphs that could describe anyone
- over-explaining obvious points
- excessive buzzwords around AI, UX, gamification, and accessibility
- exaggerated certainty about impact
- statements that imply production maturity when the artifact is a prototype
- heroic founder/startup language

When replacing AI-ish wording, prefer concrete, modest, specific language.

Example:

Bad:
> I leverage AI-driven workflows to create innovative, user-centered solutions that empower learners.

Better:
> Jeg bruker AI som en del av arbeidsflyten når det faktisk hjelper: til å utforske løsninger, bygge raskere, teste antakelser og oppdage hull jeg ellers kunne oversett.

## Preferred writing pattern

Prefer this structure:

1. Concrete situation or problem.
2. What Stian did.
3. What he learned or why it matters.
4. What this says about how he works.

Example:

> I Klar-prosjektet startet ikke designet med en ferdig appidé, men med et praktisk problem: elever som strevde med å få oversikt, prioritere og be om hjelp uten at det ble stigmatiserende. Jeg jobbet med elever og lærere gjennom workshops, prototyper og iterasjoner, og endte med en fungerende PWA som undersøker hvordan struktur, autonomistøtte og lærerinnsikt kan spille sammen. For meg er det dette som er interessant med UX: ikke bare skjermene, men hvordan en løsning blir mulig å bruke i en ekte hverdag.

## Professional mode

Professional mode should be clear, credible, and easy to scan.

Tone:

- serious but personable
- concise but not cold
- honest about student/master/prototype context
- confident about transferable skills

Should emphasize:

- design-based master project
- participatory design
- UX research and synthesis
- working prototypes
- AI-assisted development as a controlled workflow
- classroom/user insight
- ability to bridge user needs and technical implementation
- relevance to VG X: AI, user experience, prototyping, product thinking, editorial/news-adjacent interaction design

Avoid:

- excessive jokes
- too much game language
- long walls of text
- overselling the prototypes as finished products
- hiding that this is an application portfolio

## Skamløs AI-pitch mode

This mode may be more playful and self-aware. It can admit that the portfolio is doing something unusual.

Tone:

- playful, brave, slightly weird, but still coherent
- “game as interface”, not gimmick layered on top of a normal page
- should still communicate real competence and evidence
- should not become so chaotic that the user loses trust

Good framing:

- This is an experimental application experience.
- It shows how Stian thinks with systems, interfaces, AI and narrative.
- It is allowed to be a little shameless, but the evidence must be real.

Avoid:

- making the game a joke with no portfolio value
- too much text inside the world
- long duplicate scroll sections in Skamløs mode
- forcing the user to read lore before understanding the application
- “job unlocked” language; prefer “next arena”, “next mission”, “VG X portal”

## Klar framing

Klar should be treated as the strongest case.

Klar is:

- a design-based master project
- a functioning PWA prototype
- based on real teacher/pupil context
- focused on executive function support, structure, prioritization, help-seeking, autonomy support, and teacher insight
- an example of AI/product judgment when relevant, not just AI decoration

Do not collapse Klar into “Copilot made this”. The important story is Stian’s design process, user/context understanding, decisions, testing, iteration, and agentic workflow management.

Good framing:

> Klar er ikke først og fremst interessant fordi det er en app. Det er interessant fordi det prøver å gjøre et vanskelig skoleproblem konkret nok til at elever og lærere kan reagere på det, teste det og forbedre det.

## AI and agentic workflow framing

AI should be presented as a serious workflow, not magic.

Emphasize:

- Stian acts as product/design/tech lead
- agents implement, but he defines goals, constraints, acceptance criteria and quality standards
- he maintains docs, logs, project DNA, technical debt and persistent rules
- he visually and programmatically checks results
- he iterates when the agent misunderstands or overbuilds
- he uses AI to build faster, but also has to steer it critically

Avoid:

- “AI built everything for me”
- “I automated design”
- “prompt engineering” as vague buzzword
- claiming deep senior developer competence if the evidence is more about workflow, systems thinking and product direction

Good phrasing:

> Det mest interessante med agentisk utvikling er ikke at en modell kan skrive kode. Det er at kvaliteten avhenger av hvordan arbeidet rammes inn, dokumenteres, testes og korrigeres.

## UX/UI principles to align with

When editing the site, align with:

- clear information hierarchy
- fast understanding of who Stian is and why he is relevant
- progressive disclosure: summary first, details when wanted
- accessibility: keyboard, focus states, contrast, readable text, alt text
- reduced cognitive load
- visible evidence near claims
- no unnecessary duplicated content
- meaningful CTAs
- honest prototype/demo labels
- responsive layout
- readable case structure: problem, role, process, artifact, learning, relevance
- playful mode must still be usable

## Portfolio-specific constraints

Keep:

- Professional mode as a safe, sendable portfolio
- Skamløs AI-pitch as experimental/game-first experience
- Klar as its own major case/landmark
- VG X as “next arena”, not “job unlocked”
- demo access notes for Klar and related prototypes where relevant
- evidence links and screenshots
- lightbox behavior for image inspection
- keyboard/game accessibility in Skamløs mode

Avoid:

- long duplicate content in Skamløs mode
- generic AI wording
- hiding navigation
- making the site hard to understand for a recruiter
- making claims that cannot be backed by a screenshot, link, project, or short explanation

## How to edit Stian’s text

When rewriting existing copy:

1. Preserve factual meaning.
2. Reduce generic AI phrasing.
3. Add specificity where claims are vague.
4. Keep the writing in Stian’s voice.
5. Make sentences shorter when the page needs scanability.
6. Keep some personality; do not sterilize everything.
7. Avoid inventing experience or results.
8. Flag uncertain claims instead of silently strengthening them.
9. Prefer Norwegian unless the current section is clearly intended to be English.
10. Make the application feel human, not manufactured.

When an agent uses this profile, the final report should include:

- what text was changed and why
- examples of AI-markers removed
- UX/UI issues found
- UX/UI issues fixed
- remaining issues or tradeoffs
- commands/tests run
- screenshots or visual QA notes if possible
