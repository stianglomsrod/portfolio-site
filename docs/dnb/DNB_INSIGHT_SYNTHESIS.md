## 1. The core insight

The new sources turn the DNB story from a _claimed_ posture into an _examined, credentialed_ one.

Before: "Stian is an AI-first builder with system understanding" was a positioning statement backed mostly by self-description and the live Klar site.

Now: the same statement is backed by a formally examined master's thesis (design-science format), an official diploma showing graded competence in participatory design, innovation/prototyping, project management and research methods, and a working fullstack AI product (Klar) whose architecture is _argued from principles_ in the thesis.

The strategic consequence: **the DNB page should lead with evidence, not adjectives.** The strongest move is to let the artefacts (Klar, the documented workflow, the formal method) carry the positioning, with the candidate's framing kept sober and honest.

## 4. Risky / weak angles to avoid or soften

- **No student outcome claims.** The thesis is explicit: Klar was not tested with students; it is not an effect study. Never imply Klar is proven to help students or reduce stigma. Frame all such points as _designed for_, evaluated by teachers, pending student testing.
- **No senior distributed-systems / platform-engineering claim.** Nothing in the sources supports production-scale distributed systems. Keep this as growth direction only.
- **No established lower-level engineering expertise.** Curiosity about lower-level/C/sensors is a growth direction, not a current competency.
- **Don't over-cite academic specifics on the public page.** Sikt/GDPR clauses, citation lists, and methodology jargon belong in backing docs, not the hero. On-page, use plain language ("godkjent personvernprosess", "menneske-i-løkka").
- **Don't lean on self-authored prep as proof.** The exam manuscript and Q&A bank are excellent for _language and reasoning_ but are circular as evidence. Load-bearing claims must trace to the thesis, diploma, repo, or live site.
- **Avoid pure-UX framing.** The method work is a strength, but it must serve the engineering narrative, not replace it. Always land design insight on a _technical_ decision.
